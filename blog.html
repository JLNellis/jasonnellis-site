2025 · 02 Every generation blames the next medium.Radio, comics, TV, video games, streaming. Now algorithmic feeds. The format changes. The anxiety doesn't. Creator Economy 3 min ›

Yesterday I flew to London for the Children's Media Summit at BAFTA, hosted by The Children's Media Foundation. A room full of people who shape what the next generation watches, reads, and absorbs.

The most interesting part wasn't the animation pipelines or IP strategy. It was the anxiety.

Teachers spoke openly about phones in schools. They're a distraction, yes, but also real fear about kids being exposed too early to environments where grooming and predatory behavior exist. Parents and policymakers echoed similar concerns about personalization, algorithms, and kids consuming alone instead of together.

Several speakers argued we need to "bring everyone back to the television" to recreate shared cultural moments. (Remember when we were all told that TV rots your brain?)

And then an older woman stood up during Q&A and quietly reframed the whole thing.

When she was young, she said, having your head buried in a book was seen as antisocial. Lonely. Even unhealthy. Books were once blamed for pulling children away from real life. Today, we celebrate readers.

It was a useful reminder. Every generation believes the next medium is the one that will finally ruin children. Radio. Comic books. Television. Video games. Streaming. Now algorithmic feeds.

That doesn't mean the concerns aren't real. On the contrary — kids absolutely need guardrails. Platforms do need responsibility. Phones in schools are not a trivial issue.

But media evolution is cyclical. We tend to panic first, adapt second, and normalize third.

As someone who works inside the creator economy, I'm increasingly convinced that solutions won't come from one authority. Not platforms alone, not policymakers alone, not parents alone. They come from understanding the system from multiple vantage points: creators, educators, families, technologists, and yes, especially critics.

The format changes, but the human challenges don't.

Where's the line between healthy caution and cultural overcorrection? More immediately, what medium are we panicking about today that we'll quietly normalize in twenty years?

2025 · 01 Dump your algorithm. Follow people instead.AI-generated slop is flooding every discovery feed. There's a simple fix — and it changes the game for creators too. Strategy 4 min ›

Radical idea: dump your Discover, For You, and Explore feeds for a while. Go old school. Just watch content from people you actually choose to follow.

Don't think of this as a nostalgia play — it's a way to fix a growing problem with your content consumption.

As AI-generated content continues to flood every platform, it's getting harder to tell what's human and what's machine-generated. And that line is only going to blur further. The uncanny valley is shrinking fast, and "I'll just trust my instincts" doesn't work anymore.

The problem isn't the existence of AI content, but that discovery feeds are structurally optimized for volume — because, hey, ad revenue. In a twisted way, they prefer slop over substance. When the cost of producing content approaches zero, the algorithm doesn't get better taste; it just gets more inventory. Engagement becomes a numbers game, not a quality game.

Creators feel this first. When everything looks polished and abundant, the signal that actually matters — trust — gets diluted. And consumers feel it too, even if they can't articulate why. Feeds start to feel noisy, interchangeable, and strangely hollow, despite being "high quality." Your Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, and YouTube Shorts experiences all start to look the same.

Try something different

Instead of scrolling more, try engaging directly without curation. Follow creators deliberately, interact directly, and share decidedly.

Likes are fine, but they're the weakest signal. Sharing actually puts a creator's work in front of other people who trust you — and platforms register that better. Commenting adds context and depth. Even a short, thoughtful response tells a creator: "This landed with an actual person." Those signals matter as much, if not more, than passive watch time ever will.

For consumers, this shift does something subtle but important. It reduces cognitive load. Following fewer people more intentionally makes the experience calmer, slower, and more coherent. You're not being trained to react — you're choosing what you want more of.

For creators, it changes the game in quieter ways. It rewards consistency over gimmicks. Voice over velocity. Relationship over reach. When audiences engage intentionally, creators can take more creative risks without feeling like they're shouting into the void.

This isn't anti-discovery

Discovery done well is valuable — TikTok wouldn't exist without it. But until companies are properly incentivized to label synthetic content, adjust ranking systems, and stop rewarding volume, take a stand.

Find real people. Support them visibly. Be intentional with your attention.

2024 · 10 Live video still can't figure out the money.YouTube just made big promises about Live. I helped build Meta's answer to this in 2021. Here's what we learned — and why the math still doesn't work. Product 4 min ›

If you missed the Made on YouTube event, you might not know they're pushing their most ambitious update to Live yet. Creators can now stream in vertical and horizontal formats at the same time with a unified chat. AI-generated highlights become Shorts, ads can run side-by-side, and creators can rehearse privately before going live.

These changes show how quickly live video is becoming more flexible and creator-friendly — but fail to address the bigger issue. It's really hard to monetize live content.

What we built at Meta

In 2021, during the height of the pandemic, I helped build a Meta product called Super. It was an experiment in live, interactive creator monetization. The patent was filed, the product launched, and then it faded into the ether.

What matters isn't that it disappeared, but what it revealed about a category that has repeatedly struggled to prove itself. For years, companies have promised creators sustainable revenue from real-time interactions. Even Twitch, the category leader, shows how hard it is to make the math work: in 2024, Twitch made $1.8B in revenue, down from 2023. Concurrency fell to 2.37M and hours watched to 20.8B, well below pandemic highs. Even at its scale, Twitch hasn't expanded much beyond gaming or found consistent growth.

If Twitch is struggling, smaller bets like Super were always going to face headwinds.

What Super taught me

Super's backdrop was the popularity of Clubhouse and Houseparty — remember those? — and every tech company racing to respond. Meta wanted a foothold in live, and Super was our bet. The idea was straightforward: let creators host lightweight live shows, customize the look, and offer a menu of monetization they could tailor to their audience.

We built fast, and we built desktop-first. That sped up learning, but limited adoption because creators and fans wanted to be on their phones. We also tried to build for "creators" as a whole, which meant no single group felt especially served. We should have built for a niche, and grown from there.

Then there were incentives. Early traction looked promising, but much of it was paid for. Once we stopped funding usage, activity dropped to near zero. Subsidized growth proved fragile, and ultimately sent too many false signals.

The product was sunset, but the lessons stuck: timing creates urgency but not product-market fit. Speed matters, but so does choosing the right platform. Niche focus beats building for everyone. And incentives can drive activity, but rarely sustain it.

Super didn't solve the puzzle. Neither has Twitch, despite its scale. Live video continues to draw experiments because the promises of community, intimacy, and connection are powerful. But turning that into durable, profitable revenue remains one of the hardest challenges in tech.

So to YouTube, I say — bon chance. You might need it.

2024 · 09 Time zones aren't chaos. They're structure.How I turned working across multiple time zones into a productivity advantage — with kids in tow. Leadership 3 min ›

If you have ever worked across multiple time zones, you know the default mode: chaos. Late-night Slack pings, early morning emails, and that constant feeling that you are always behind.

The truth is: time zones don't have to be chaos. They are structure, if you design for them. Here's how I make multiple time zones work while traveling abroad with family.

Morning: Async check-ins (30 minutes)

I start by reviewing messages, recording short updates, and setting priorities for my team. No calls, no live debates. Just clarity for the inbox before they even wake up.

Midday: Deep work (4–5 hours)

This is the gift of time zones: long, uninterrupted stretches perfect for strategy, writing, or product work that actually needs my brain at full power. Get the kids to their daycare or camp, and get going.

One of my favorite examples was during a push for SuperBam while we were in Spain. The U.S. team was still asleep, and my kids were momentarily occupied with Lego towers and morning cartoons. That left me a block of pure focus. In those hours, I drafted the product strategy notes that became the foundation for an API integration plan with one of our vendors. When the team woke up, they found a fully fleshed-out proposal waiting for them, instead of half-finished thoughts in Slack. By the time we got on our first call that afternoon, decisions were easier and everyone moved faster because the groundwork was already set.

That rhythm — quiet build first, alignment later — saved us days on the timeline.

Afternoon and evening: Alignment and live touchpoints (2–3 hours)

By the time the U.S. team comes online, I have already had a full workday. I keep this block for 1:1s, team syncs, or big decisions. They get me focused, I get them fresh.

It's also when I can connect dots for both sides of my life. I once wrapped a product review at 6:30, closed the laptop, and immediately went outside with my son to chase lizards around a garden wall in Nice before dinner. The overlap is both professional and personal — which is the real win.

Time zones are not a curse. They can be a productivity multiplier — when you build your effort around them.

2024 · 08 Hulu made my career. Now it's really gone.I almost turned down the job. I became employee 45. I owe most of what followed to nearly five years there. Reflection 3 min ›

I knew this moment was coming, and yet I'm still a bit heartbroken.

When I was 23 years old, I moved to Los Angeles thinking I would pursue acting. About a minute after I landed at LAX, the 2007 WGA Strike started, and any hopes of finding my way onto a TV or film set evaporated.

Then, a friend referred me to the Friedman Personnel Agency, and their first gig was to send me to a company no one had heard of yet — Hulu. I became employee number 45.

What most people don't know: I actually declined the job the first time it was offered. The folks at Friedman couldn't seem to accept that I wanted to think about it for an evening before I took the role. So when they said "it's our way or no way," I basically said "no way!" and hung up the phone. Fortunately, Kay Gabaldon and Andrew Lin called me and set me straight, and the rest is history.

Despite almost shooting myself in the foot for no good reason, I owe my career — and most of the jobs I've had since — to the nearly five years I spent at Hulu. Learning from people like Jason Kilar, Eric Feng, Christina Lee, Andy Forssell, and David Baron. Building the beginnings of careers alongside incredible people like Ben Collins, Chris Cuyler, and Sara Escobar. I still think of it as my MBA where I was paid.

When news hit of Disney's full absorption of the company, it was only a matter of time before they combined Disney+ and Hulu into a single service. I understand the Hulu brand itself will still exist in some form. But the days of Super Bowl ads, launching Hulu Plus, and spinning up Hulu Japan are long gone.

That's okay — it's not important that things stay the same, especially for companies. But I'm bummed that the place where a really pivotal chapter in my professional and personal life happened is now really, truly gone.

2024 · 06 We moved to Europe to change our lives.Two kids, two careers, one giant leap. What happened next was chaotic, joyful, and clarifying. Life 2 min ›

We moved to Europe — not for a vacation, but to change our lives. Two kids, two careers, and one giant leap.

This move was 18 months in the making. We were deep in the process of securing long-stay visas when the birth of our second son hit pause on the plan. Now we've finally landed in France — but instead of settling into a routine, we decided to keep moving. We're exploring Europe as a family, one train ride, one city, one new language at a time.

So far: Mallorca, Munich, and the Austrian Alps. We've seen old friends who live here and welcomed friends from the US who came to travel with us. Our younger son learned to crawl and pull himself up. Our older son started making friends with kids who don't speak his language. And we've caught our breath in skylines across the continent.

Through it all, I'm still leading product strategy. My wife is building her business from hotel lobbies and sunny terraces. Our boys are learning through experience what no classroom could offer.

It's chaotic and joyful and, somehow, incredibly clarifying. Relocation didn't shrink my ambitions — it expanded them. I want my work to matter globally. I want to build products that resonate beyond borders. And this chapter — full of motion, risk, and reinvention — is teaching me how to do exactly that.

We're living in a vast world with different wants and needs, and too often we build for a narrow segment of people.
